Member of a 5-person team that accepted a 2-day Challenge to develop a concept for a software that would help a fictional National Firefight Division managing fire operations around rural areas. I mainly contributed to the information architecture of this prototype and promoted some activities/methods to help align the team on solving the problems on each stage.

<!-- with activities to synthesize knowledge, generate ideas and validating the wireflows. -->

The methodology used was loosely based on [Google Design Sprint](https://www.gv.com/sprint/). 

Firstly, we tried to **understand** the problem. We collected information primarily by interviewing firefighters. Then we described the problem: its objective, its needs, the steps taken in a firefight operation, the people involved with it (<a href="#research" class="ref">1</a>).

Then, we started to **define** and **sketch** the system. Define its goals and target users, the main features, its limitations, use-scenarios and personas. We also performed a group exercise of brainstorming the system UI (a _solution sketch_), then we discussed and listed those ideas and created a moodboard to complement them (<a href="#sketch" class="ref">2</a>).

    <img id="sketch" src="/imgs/fire-ops/sketch.jpg" alt="">

We had to **decide** what we would prototype. We used a new concept as the basis for the solution that joined our best ideas.

As all of us had attended a lecture about airplane control design, we had learned that though an aircraft cockpit control is complex, only a few indicators and controls are important and common to all kinds of aircrafts.

In our project, we thought about which would be the most important indicators when managing a fire operation.

We selected the following three items (<a href="#indicators" class="ref">3</a>):
- The line of fire (power and direction);
- Number and location of endangered people;
- How many (human) resources have been allocated.

The next step was to **prototype** our solution. We decided what features should go on the main screen, and which ones on secondary ones. We were divided in two groups to start “pair-prototyping” the different screens on Figma. While the prototype was evolving, one of us would check if the experience flow was ok. Whenever a problem or a new idea was found we would discuss it and redo/implement it. The elements we agreed that wouldn’t be changing another member would start a hi-fi version of them.

When we finished the main flows of the prototype, it was time to **test & adjust** it. We couldn’t find any potential users available, so we performed usability tests with people present at the event. Due to the feedback received, we had to redo some screens and find a middle ground between too much text and little explanatory screens.

The **solution proposed** assumed that this National Firefight Division had access to all the data available by the forces involved (Firefighters, Medical Services, ..). The main features are:
- a small set of indicators to quickly know the state of the operation, and
- system suggested actions.

The first feature was already mentioned. The second one, is that the system should suggests which actions to take accordingly the data gathered. For example, based on location, number of villages around, fire and wind direction, the system suggests the number and type of units to be sent in order to close the traffic, evacuate the surroundings, and help firefighting (see <a href="#prototype" class="ref">4</a>). Note that many of these suggestions do not need a “highly intelligent system”, it can start with a set of basic rules and constraints that trigger warnings, that would grow while developing it.

## Conclusion and Learning Points

These were very tiring but fun days, some of the highlights of being part of this project were:
- Learning about Google Design Sprint methodology, and that its website is a good resource for unblocking exercises;
- It is important to keep an open and curious mind, as solutions in other areas could guide us solving our problems;
- Dealing with people under stress due a very short timeline was hard to handle: on the last day some of us were working by themselves to improve the prototype without the group's agreement, which led to frustration when it had to be changed substantially.
